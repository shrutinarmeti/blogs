Understanding Gamma Squeezes: The Mechanics Behind Rapid Stock Price Surges

Explore the concept of gamma squeezes, how they can fuel rapid stock price increases, and the factors that contribute to their occurrence in 2026.

In recent years, the term "gamma squeeze" has entered the everyday vocabulary of retail and institutional investors alike, especially as markets have become more influenced by derivatives trading. You may have heard about it during the GameStop saga or other high-profile market events where certain stocks skyrocketed in value seemingly overnight. But what exactly is a gamma squeeze, and how does it happen? In this article, we'll break it down in simple terms, exploring its mechanics, risks, and the dynamics that can lead to these sudden price surges.

What is a Gamma Squeeze?

To understand a gamma squeeze, it's essential to first understand some basic concepts in options trading—particularly the role of market makers and the concept of gamma.

Options 101: Calls, Puts, and Market Makers

An option is a financial contract that gives its owner the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a specified price (the strike price) before a specified date. A call option gives you the right to buy an asset, while a put option gives you the right to sell it.

Market makers play a crucial role in options markets by providing liquidity. They do this by standing ready to buy or sell options to ensure there’s always a counterparty for traders. However, market makers don’t usually hold onto these contracts—they hedge their positions to avoid taking significant risks. This hedging activity is where gamma squeezes come into play.

Understanding Gamma in Options

Gamma is a measure of how much the delta of an option (its sensitivity to the underlying stock’s price movement) changes as the stock price moves. Put simply, gamma represents the rate of change of delta. If delta is the speed of an option’s price movement relative to the underlying stock, gamma is the acceleration.

When traders buy call options, market makers who sell those options often hedge their exposure by purchasing the underlying stock. As the stock price rises, the delta of the call options increases, meaning the market makers need to buy even more stock to maintain their hedge. This creates a feedback loop where the stock’s rising price leads to more buying by market makers, which further drives up the stock price. This phenomenon is what’s known as a gamma squeeze.

How Does a Gamma Squeeze Unfold?

Let’s take a closer look at the mechanics of a gamma squeeze through a step-by-step example:

  1. High Options Volume: Retail traders, hedge funds, or a combination of investors start buying a large number of call options on a particular stock. In many cases, these are out-of-the-money (OTM) call options, which are cheaper but more speculative.
  2. Market Maker Hedging: When a market maker sells these call options, they acquire a short position in the option. To hedge this risk, they buy shares of the underlying stock.
  3. Stock Price Rises: If the underlying stock starts to rally, the delta of the call options rises. To adjust their hedges, market makers must buy more shares of the stock.
  4. Feedback Loop: The added buying pressure from market makers can push the stock price higher, causing the cycle to repeat. As the stock climbs closer to the strike prices of the OTM options, the delta increases even more, requiring market makers to buy even more shares.
  5. Explosion in Demand: This feedback loop can cause a sharp, rapid increase in the stock price—often catching short sellers off guard and leading to additional short covering, which can further amplify the rally.

Why Do Gamma Squeezes Happen?

Gamma squeezes typically occur in stocks or other assets where there is a combination of high speculation, low liquidity, and a large amount of options trading activity. Several factors increase the likelihood of a gamma squeeze:

  • High Short Interest: Stocks with a high percentage of shares sold short are more vulnerable to short squeezes, which can amplify the effects of a gamma squeeze.
  • Low Float: Stocks with a limited number of shares available for trading are more prone to sharp price movements due to supply constraints.
  • Retail Participation: The rise of commission-free trading platforms has enabled retail investors to collectively buy large amounts of call options, often targeting specific stocks.
  • Social Media Hype: Platforms like Reddit, Twitter, and Discord can rapidly amplify interest in a particular stock, creating a snowball effect as more traders pile in.

How Gamma Squeezes Differ from Short Squeezes

Gamma squeezes are often confused with short squeezes, but they’re distinct phenomena. Here’s a comparison:

Aspect Gamma Squeeze Short Squeeze
Primary Driver Market makers hedging call options Short sellers covering their positions
Trigger Large purchases of call options Rapid increase in stock price, forcing short sellers to buy back shares
Feedback Loop Buying by market makers pushes the stock price higher, increasing delta Short covering adds buying pressure, pushing the stock price higher
Common Targets Stocks with high options activity and low liquidity Stocks with high short interest

The Risks of a Gamma Squeeze

While gamma squeezes can lead to massive gains for some traders, they also come with significant risks:

  • High Volatility: The same forces that drive a stock’s price upward during a gamma squeeze can cause it to collapse just as quickly once the buying pressure subsides.
  • Speculative Nature: Gamma squeezes are often driven by speculation rather than fundamentals. This makes them risky for long-term investors who may be left holding the bag after the price corrects.
  • Potential for Losses: Both buyers of call options and short sellers can face significant losses if the market moves against them during a squeeze.

Lessons for Investors

Gamma squeezes are a reminder of how complex and interconnected financial markets can be, especially in the age of advanced trading algorithms and social media. Here are some key takeaways for investors:

  • Understand the Risks: Before trading options, make sure you fully understand the risks involved, including the potential for rapid and unexpected price movements.
  • Stick to Your Strategy: Avoid chasing "hot" stocks or trying to time the market during a gamma squeeze. These events are often unpredictable and can lead to significant losses.
  • Be Cautious with Leverage: High leverage can amplify both gains and losses, making it especially risky during volatile market conditions.

Conclusion

Gamma squeezes are one of the more fascinating and dramatic phenomena in modern financial markets, but they underscore the risks and complexities of trading in a world increasingly dominated by derivatives and algorithmic strategies. While they can provide opportunities for quick profits, they are not for the faint of heart and require a clear understanding of market mechanics and risk management strategies.
