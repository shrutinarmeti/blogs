How to Use Earnings Yield to Identify Undervalued Stocks

Learn how earnings yield can help you evaluate stock investments, identify undervalued opportunities, and compare them against other asset classes for smarter decisions.

When it comes to evaluating whether a stock is worth your hard-earned money, the price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio often takes center stage. But there’s another metric that deserves just as much attention: earnings yield. This powerful, yet surprisingly underutilized, financial metric flips the P/E ratio on its head and provides a different perspective for assessing stocks. In this post, we’ll break down what earnings yield is, how to calculate it, and how you can use it to identify undervalued opportunities in the market.

What is Earnings Yield?

At its core, earnings yield is the inverse of the P/E ratio. It measures the percentage of a company’s earnings relative to its stock price. While the P/E ratio tells you how much you’re paying for $1 of earnings, earnings yield tells you how much earnings you’re getting for every $1 you invest in the stock.

The formula for earnings yield is straightforward:

Earnings Yield = (Earnings Per Share / Stock Price) × 100

For example, if a stock has an earnings per share (EPS) of $5 and a current stock price of $50, its earnings yield is:

Earnings Yield = ($5 / $50) × 100 = 10%

This means the stock is generating a 10% return on your investment in terms of its earnings.

Why Should You Pay Attention to Earnings Yield?

Unlike the P/E ratio, which can sometimes be distorted by high stock prices, earnings yield provides an easier way to compare investments across asset classes. Here’s why it’s important:

  • Comparison with Bonds: Earnings yield can be directly compared to the yield on bonds or other fixed-income securities to assess whether stocks offer a more attractive return. For example, if a company’s earnings yield is 8% and 10-year Treasury bonds offer a yield of 4%, the stock may be relatively more attractive.
  • Focus on Earnings: Unlike other valuation metrics, such as price-to-sales or price-to-book ratios, earnings yield focuses specifically on profitability, making it useful for identifying companies with strong earnings relative to their market price.
  • Valuation Indicator: A higher earnings yield may indicate that a stock is undervalued, while a lower earnings yield might suggest overvaluation. However, it’s important to consider this metric in context with other factors.

How to Use Earnings Yield in Stock Analysis

Now that we understand what earnings yield is, let’s look at some practical ways to use it in your investment decisions.

1. Compare with Fixed-Income Investments

One of the most effective uses of earnings yield is to compare it with the yield on a risk-free investment, such as U.S. Treasury bonds. This approach is often referred to as the Fed Model. If the earnings yield of a stock or index is higher than the bond yield, it may suggest that the stock is undervalued relative to bonds.

Asset Yield Interpretation
10-Year Treasury Bond 4% Risk-free return
S&P 500 Earnings Yield 5.5% Stocks may offer better potential returns
Stock XYZ Earnings Yield 8% Potentially undervalued stock

Of course, this is a simplified analysis. Stocks carry greater risk than bonds, so the higher yield should compensate for that added risk.

2. Screen for Undervalued Stocks

Earnings yield is a handy metric for screening undervalued stocks. Look for companies with higher earnings yields compared to their industry peers or the broader market. Be cautious, though—an unusually high earnings yield might signal a “value trap,” where the stock is cheap for a good reason, such as declining earnings or industry headwinds.

3. Use in Conjunction with Other Metrics

While earnings yield is a valuable tool, it shouldn’t be used in isolation. Combine it with other metrics like the P/E ratio, price-to-book ratio, or return on equity to get a more comprehensive view of a company’s financial health and potential for growth.

Advantages and Limitations of Earnings Yield

Like any financial metric, earnings yield has its benefits and drawbacks.

Advantages:

  • Flexibility: Can be used to compare stocks with bonds and other investment options.
  • Simplicity: Easy to calculate and understand.
  • Focus on Fundamentals: Based on actual earnings, making it a reliable indicator of profitability.

Limitations:

  • Ignores Growth: Earnings yield doesn’t account for future growth potential, so it’s best used for mature companies with stable earnings.
  • Vulnerability to Manipulation: Earnings can be manipulated through accounting practices, potentially distorting the metric.
  • Market Conditions: During periods of economic uncertainty, even stocks with high earnings yields may underperform.

Conclusion: A Powerful Tool in the Right Hands

Earnings yield is an invaluable metric that can simplify the process of evaluating investment opportunities, especially when compared across asset classes like stocks and bonds. However, as with any financial metric, context is key. Use earnings yield as part of a broader, well-rounded investment analysis to make more informed and confident decisions.

By adding earnings yield to your investing toolkit, you’ll gain a sharper edge in identifying undervalued stocks and making smarter choices in an ever-changing market landscape.
